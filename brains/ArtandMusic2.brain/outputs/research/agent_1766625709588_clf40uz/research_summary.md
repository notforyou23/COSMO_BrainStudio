## Summary

This research identifies a pragmatic pathway into the under-documented area of Black British experimental composition/improvisation from the 1980s–2000s: start with composer-facing catalogues that provide verified works lists and performance/commission metadata, then expand outward to venue/label documentation and longform interviews that capture methods, networks, and reception often missing from conventional bibliographies. The British Music Collection is highlighted as a strong entry point for practitioners who sit between composed “new music,” free improvisation, and cross-disciplinary performance—exemplified by dossiers on Tunde Jegede and Elaine Mitchener that can be mined for named works and collaborators.

On the primary-source side, the British Library emerges as the central UK repository suggesting immediate, concrete archival work. Its public-facing collection narratives (e.g., Samuel Coleridge-Taylor at 150; Beyond the Bassline exhibition) demonstrate both depth (autograph manuscripts, printed music, recordings, publishers’ archives like Boosey & Hawkes) and a current institutional focus on Black British music histories. However, practical access constraints remain important: post-cyberattack recovery means discovery and requesting can require interim catalogues, staff contact, and advance appointments for Sound & Vision materials.

Finally, the research points to living scholarly and practitioner networks that can rapidly generate further leads, especially the University of Westminster’s Black Music Research Unit (with a public directory of researchers) and curated interviews/conversations (e.g., Barbican’s Mitchener–Alero discussion) that articulate experimental practice as research and name adjacent collaborators, ensembles, and curators. A key gap is that standard academic bibliographies alone may not adequately capture experimental, cross-genre compositional activity; the most fruitful next steps combine archives, institutional hubs, and scene documentation to triangulate works, events, recordings, and critical discourse.

## Key Findings

1. Finding 1: The British Music Collection provides composer dossiers that function as reliable “primary-data launchpads” (works lists, stylistic framing, and sometimes commissioning/performance histories) for Black British experimental figures bridging composition and improvisation, including Tunde Jegede and Elaine Mitchener.

2. Finding 2: The British Library holds major primary sources for Black British composers across centuries—e.g., for Samuel Coleridge‑Taylor it holds autograph manuscripts, hundreds of printed scores, and early recordings, with additional items still surfacing through ongoing cataloguing of the Boosey & Hawkes archive.

3. Finding 3: British Library access and discovery may require nonstandard workflows in the near term: interim catalogues (notably for Sound and Moving Image), direct reference staff support, and advance appointments for Listening and Viewing services while systems continue recovering post-cyberattack.

4. Finding 4: Recent institutional programming (British Library’s Beyond the Bassline, 2024) evidences an active curatorial framework and partner network that can be leveraged to identify materials, experts, and contemporary interpretive lenses around Black music in Britain (including classical/experimental lineages).

5. Finding 5: Actionable scholarly contact pathways exist now via the University of Westminster Black Music Research Unit (public researcher directory with contacts) and via longform interviews (e.g., Barbican conversation featuring Elaine Mitchener and Rebekah Alero) that document experimental vocal/compositional methods and collaborator networks.
